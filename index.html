About me

I want to be a software/electrical/computer engineer one day and maybe even start my own company. I've started making little robots and systems and programming them a few years ago and started going deeper into web development and computer science. I write a few dozen lines of code everyday and every so often I attend huge programming events called hackathons where I spend 24-48 hours writing an app or building a website.

Ever since I was a kid, I wanted to build stuff. I played with Legos and fantasized about being a scientist. Plus, it's just fun building stuff. I want to help the world and make it a better place. The feeling is also great when you finally finish a project you've been working on for a year and finally release it to all your friends around the world.

I like to lead teams and stay relaxed under pressure. When I built my school's robotics website, I was the lead developer. I around 80,000 lines of code and directed others to build certain parts and had to deal with our manager as well. At hackathons, some say that I am ambitious and work hard building out our apps and websites for a presentation to thousands. Other times, people say that I'm lazy due to the fact that I do not like completing projects that I deem boring or spend too much time with and do not provide a good learning source.

Last year, my team and I won the Best Use of Data award from Dublin High School's entrepreneurship project showcase for Rapiflash which was a piece of hardware along with predictive algorithms written in Python and a back end API written in Ruby on Rails as well as a bunch of API wrappers that were written in Python, Ruby, and Javascript. It predicted chances of a flood in your area and sends a warning if the chance is high enough. This summer, my friend and I teamed up for an adult hackathon (we were the only high school students) called the Water/Energy Nexus Hackathon in which we were tasked to build and write applications that is supposed to save California from their drought. We ended up creating Lata Wata Data that used an Arduino Uno and some home made water sensors to detect moisture in soil to gather agricultural data through the use of an API made in Ruby on Rails. There was also a companion app that received that data and displayed it in a more readable way than using the JSON data from the API. We won runner up for that out of a few dozen teams.

Airtar

Air guitar irl

At HackingEDU, we (Jesse Liang, Nathan Lo, Derrick Ng, Giang Duong, and me) created Airtar in 36 hours. Using XCode7, C++, the Leapmotion, two Myos and the hardware's SDKs, we managed to develop a pretty accurate air guitar. The code is open sourced on Github.

Solar/Hydrogen Powered Car Prototype

A Prototype for a Car

In our Principles of Engineering class, we had to create a prototype for a solar/hydrogen powered vehicle using one motor and anything else we could find in the room. We built a vehicle with 2 wheels unlike everyone else and used gears to raise the vehicle's efficiency. Our efficiency was around 30%. I built the whole vehicle, tested it, recorded measurements, as well as calculate the power and efficiency of our vehicle.

Course: POE
